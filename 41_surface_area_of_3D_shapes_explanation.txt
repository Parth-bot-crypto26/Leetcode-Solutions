===================================================================
LEETCODE 892: SURFACE AREA OF 3D SHAPES
===================================================================

[PROBLEM DESCRIPTION]
You are given an n x n grid where each value v = grid[i][j] represents a 
tower of v cubes placed on top of cell (i, j). Any directly adjacent cubes 
are glued to each other, forming several irregular 3D shapes.

Return the total surface area of the resulting shapes. Both top and bottom 
faces count toward the surface area.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
[METHODOLOGY: LOCALIZED CARDINAL CONTRIBUTION SCAN (O(N²))]
-------------------------------------------------------------------

A brute-force calculation that counts individual cube sides and tries to cross-check 
global overlaps will easily run into messy indexing bugs. The optimal strategy is 
to evaluate the surface area contribution of each tower cell (i, j) independently.

For any given tower of height 'v':

1. HORIZONTAL SURFACE PLANES (TOP & BOTTOM):
   - As long as the tower height is greater than zero ('v > 0'), it will expose 
     exactly 1 top face to the sky and 1 bottom face to the ground.
   - Action: 'total += 2;'

2. VERTICAL SIDE PLANES (CARDINAL NEIGHBORS):
   - A tower has 4 vertical side exposures: North, South, East, and West.
   - The surface area exposed on a given side depends completely on its neighbor's 
     height. If a neighboring tower is shorter, the difference in height represents 
     the vertical side wall surface area exposed to the air.
   - Formula: exposed_wall = max(0, v - neighbor_height)
   - If the neighbor is out of bounds (matrix edge), the 'neighbor_height' is treated 
     as 0, meaning the entire vertical face of height 'v' is exposed.

3. DUAL-COUNT PREVENTION:
   - Using 'max(0, v - neighbor)' means a cell only logs its own *protruding* surface 
     area. If the neighbor is taller, the expression yields 0. The exposed wall will 
     be handled symmetrically when the loop moves to that neighbor's coordinate.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
[DRY RUN EXAMPLE]
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Input: grid = [[1, 2], [3, 4]] | n = 2
Let's evaluate Cell (0, 0) where v = 1:

- Top/Bottom contribution: v > 0 -> total += 2 (total = 2)
- North Neighbor: Out of bounds -> north = 0.
                  Exposed: max(0, 1 - 0) = 1 -> total += 1 (total = 3)
- South Neighbor: grid[1][0] = 3. 
                  Exposed: max(0, 1 - 3) = 0 -> total += 0 (total = 3)
- West Neighbor:  Out of bounds -> west = 0.
                  Exposed: max(0, 1 - 0) = 1 -> total += 1 (total = 4)
- East Neighbor:  grid[0][1] = 2.
                  Exposed: max(0, 1 - 2) = 0 -> total += 0 (total = 4)

Cell (0, 0) contributes exactly 4 units to the global surface area.

-------------------------------------------------------------------
[COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS]
-------------------------------------------------------------------

Time Complexity: O(n^2)
- Explanation: Where 'n' is the row/column dimension of the grid. The nested loop 
  visits each cell coordinate in the grid exactly once. Within the inner block, the 
  algorithm performs a fixed number of lookups and basic arithmetic maximum/subtraction 
  operations. The execution time scales completely linearly with the total number of cells.

Space Complexity: O(1)
- Explanation: The algorithm executes entirely in-place. Auxiliary space allocation is 
  restricted to local scalar primitive variables ('n', 'total', 'v', 'north', etc.), 
  ensuring a clean, absolute constant memory profile.
